The concept may be understood by comprehending the difference between the number of degrees that the airplane has turned over the ground versus the number of degrees that the airplane has turned in the air. As an example, assume the airplane is exactly crosswind, meaning directly at a point that is \(90^{\circ}\) to the straight- lined ground reference. In this example, if the wind requires a \(10^{\circ}\) wind correction angle (for this example, this is a left turn with the crosswind from the left), the airplane would be at a heading that is \(10^{\circ}\) ahead when directly over the \(90^{\circ}\) ground reference point. In other words, the first \(90^{\circ}\) track over the ground would result in a heading change of \(100^{\circ}\) and the last \(90^{\circ}\) track over the ground would result in \(80^{\circ}\) of heading change.
