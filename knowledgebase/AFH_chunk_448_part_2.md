At low- speed cruise, the true airspeed is still high, but the shock wave does not move as far aft as it does in high- speed cruise. The separated flow behind the shock wave acts over a larger portion of the chord, which leads to a more significant effect on aircraft control.

The altitude at which an airplane flying at \(\mathrm{M_{MO}}\) would experience buffeting with any increase in AOA determines the absolute or aerodynamic ceiling. This is the altitude where:

- If an airplane flew any faster, it would exceed \(\mathrm{M_{MO}}\) leading to high-speed Mach buffet.- If an airplane flew any slower, it would require an angle of attack leading to low-speed Mach buffet.

This region of the airplane's flight envelope is known as "coffin corner." Conceivably, a buffet could be the first indication of an issue at altitude, and pilots should understand the cause of any buffet in order to respond appropriately.
