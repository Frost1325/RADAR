These direct control inputs determine the airplane's ability to climb/descend or accelerate/decelerate. In contrast, changes in AGL-altitude and groundspeed are affected by "external" factors, such as varying terrain elevation and wind, which the pilot cannot alter. Of course, the pilot should manipulate the airplane's energy in such way as to minimize any risks associated with terrain or wind. For example, the pilot may seek to manipulate energy state so as to maximize the airplane's energy gains and minimize energy loses when faced with rising terrain. A safer heading may also be an option.

Once airborne, the airplane gains energy from the force of engine thrust (T) and it loses energy from aerodynamic drag (D). The difference between energy in and out (T-D) is the net change, which determines whether total mechanical energy—stored as altitude and airspeed—increases, decreases, or remains the same.
