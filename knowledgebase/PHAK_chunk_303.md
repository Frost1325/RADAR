A more important advantage is that a very smooth, compound curved, aerodynamic structure made from composites reduces drag. This is the main reason sailplane designers switched from metal and wood to composites in the 1960s. In aircraft, the use of composites reduces drag for the Cirrus

and Columbia line of production aircraft, leading to their high performance despite their fixed landing gear. Composites also help mask the radar signature of "stealth" aircraft designs, such as the B- 2 and the F- 22. Today, composites can be found in aircraft as varied as gliders to most new helicopters.

Lack of corrosion is a third advantage of composites. Boeing is designing the 787, with its all- composite fuselage, to have both a higher pressure differential and higher humidity in the cabin than previous airliners. Engineers are no longer as concerned about corrosion from moisture condensation on the hidden areas of the fuselage skins, such as behind insulation blankets. This should lead to lower long- term maintenance costs for the airlines.
